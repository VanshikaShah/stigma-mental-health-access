Establishing the Problem Potential Driver Striving for Improvement

Stigma as the hidden barrier to mental health care.

Using Healthy Minds Study survey data, we explore how stigma keeps students from getting the support they need. We highlight symptom prevalence, the care gap, and actions that could improve access.

What you’ll see

  • Establishing the Problem: Reporting the prevalence of anxiety and depression across severity levels conditions among college students, and the extent of existing treatment gaps
  • Identifying a Potential Driver: Which barriers dominate, why stigma stands out, and how different demographic groups are affected
  • Striving for Improvement: Actionable strategies for both students in managing various lifestyle factors and institutions in fostering more supportive environments to narrow current treatment gaps

01 | Establishing the Problem: Symptoms, Severity, and Service Use

How prevalent are anxiety and depression symptoms, and what do current levels of service utilization look like?

Let’s start by looking at prevalence and severity, then compare need versus utilization to show where the treatment gap begins.

Prevalence of Anxiety and Depression

Each icon represents 1% of students, with over one-third of students (38%) reported anxiety, depression, or both.

Key takeaway A meaningful share of students don’t experience anxiety or depression in isolation—they report both, alongside smaller groups reporting only one condition. This overlap matters because students facing multiple symptom types may need more support and clearer pathways into treatment. Mental health needs are often layered, so services shouldn’t assume a one-condition-at-a-time experience.

How severe are symptoms among students?

These charts break anxiety and depression into severity levels (minimal -> severe).

Key takeaway Most students report minimal or mild symptoms, but there is still a noticeable portion experiencing moderate-to-severe anxiety or depression levels that are more likely to interfere with daily functioning and signal a need for support. The “tail” matters, moderate and severe symptoms are common enough that access barriers (like stigma) can impact many students, not just a small minority.

Where the care gap begins: need vs treatment

This flow chart shows how students move from their diagnosis group (anxiety only, depression only, or both) to whether they perceive a need for treatment, and then to their treatment status (therapy, medication, both, or none). It helps separate two gaps: not recognizing a need vs. recognizing a need but still not receiving care.

Key takeaway A major drop-off happens before treatment even begins, many students fall into the “no perceived need” pathway, which means symptoms don’t always translate into help-seeking. Even among students who do perceive a need, a visible portion still ends up with no treatment, showing that barriers persist after the decision to seek help. Closing this gap requires both increasing mental health awareness (so need is recognized) and reducing barriers that prevent students from following through with treatment.
02 | Identifying a Potential Driver: Why Stigma Stands Out

Stigma-related barriers, both stemming from internal and external factors, remain prominent.

This section focuses on reported barriers to care and highlights the role stigma plays relative to other obstacles like time, cost, and access.

Barriers to care (No Services vs Reduced Services to care)

Categorized Barriers Leading to No or Reduced Mental Health Service Usage.

Key takeaway Across both groups of students who received no services and those who received reduced services, stigma-related barriers remain highly reported, suggesting that the treatment gap is not explained by logistics alone. Structural issues matter, but concerns like privacy, self-reliance, and perceived judgment continue to shape whether students seek and continue care. Access expands treatment only partially unless stigma-related barriers are addressed.

How prominent is stigma?

This chart shows the top 10 most common barriers students reported when they did not receive care (NS) or received fewer services than needed (HS) in the past year. Use the view toggle to compare NS, HS, or Both, and note how barriers cluster into stigma-related, structural, and financial categories.

Key takeaway The most frequently reported barriers are not just “hard” constraints like time and cost, many are mindset or stigma-related, such as viewing stress as normal, preferring to handle problems alone, or relying on informal support. Closing the gap isn’t only about adding more appointments, stigma and “I’ll handle it myself” thinking often stop students from seeking care in the first place.

Treatment gap by demographics

Use the filters to focus the view on a specific context. Start with Service Access and Condition Type, then toggle Split by Sexuality and Split by Race to compare groups. Color indicates the share of students reporting demographic fit barriers, helping you see where fit concerns align with larger gaps.

Key takeaway Across groups, perceived need is consistently higher than care received, creating a visible treatment gap across all groups that further varies by sexuality and race/ethnicity. In several subgroups, the gap remains large even when need is high, suggesting that access barriers persist beyond symptoms alone. Considering sexuality, members of the LGBTQ+ community present a higher perceived need for treatment, although access to care is also higher, leading to relatively comparable treatment gaps to heterosexuals; additionally, LGBTQ+ individuals tend to report more barriers associated with identity fit compared to heterosexuals, suggestive of fears of stigmatization. Considering race, non-white students tend to show larger treatment gaps than white students, with Asian students typically reporting the most barriers associated with identity fit, potentially relating to cultural stigmatization. Thus, differences in the treatment gap across demographics point to the importance of culturally and identity responsive services, not just increasing overall capacity.
03A | Striving for Improvement: Institutionally-Led Methods to Reduce Stigma

Campus context that shapes stigma and the treatment gap

Student routines matter, but campuses also influence whether support feels accessible or socially “safe” to seek. Campus sentiment shapes the prevalence of stigma and associated treatment gap, suggesting an opportunity for institutions to foster more supportive environments. The next few views show institution-level patterns of how academic pressure relates to unmet need, and how higher stigma environments are linked to different coping behaviors.

Academic Pressure and the Treatment Gap Across Institutions

Each point represents an institution. The x-axis can be switched (e.g., competitiveness), and the y-axis shows the treatment gap (percent perceiving need minus percent treated).

Key takeaway Across institutions, higher academic pressure measures tend to align with larger treatment gaps, suggesting that campus climate can shape whether need turns into care. The spread of points also shows that some schools achieve lower gaps even at similar pressure levels, which hints at modifiable institutional practices beyond student characteristics alone. Institutional context can widen or narrow the care gap—pressure-heavy environments may need stronger, more visible support pathways.

How Coping Behaviors Vary with Stigma Levels Across Institutions

This view links the average number of reported stigma barriers at an institution to the share of students reporting different coping behaviors. Use the slider to focus on specific stigma ranges and compare trends across behaviors.

Key takeaway As institutional stigma increases, coping patterns tend to shift. Generally, students lean more toward self-reliance and informal forms of support, which may be potentially ineffective methods for addressing significant mental health concerns. Meanwhile, institutional engagement largely remains flat, suggesting that there is much room for institutions to encourage service access and usage. Simply offering services is not the same as students knowing about or feeling able to use them; reducing stigma is an institutional responsibility, and campus climate can heavily influence whether students seek formal help or cope alone.
03B | Striving for Improvement: Student-Driven / Lifestyle-Related Strategies for Prevention

Everyday student habits that move with mental health and help-seeking

Beyond systematic changes, smaller everyday habits and lifestyle choices can help students better manage their mental health in healthy ways.

What Lifestyle Choices Worsen vs Improve Symptoms?

A high-level view of how lifestyle and substance use relate to anxiety and depression outcomes in this survey.Here, PHQ-9 is a standard depression symptom score, and GAD-7 is a standard anxiety symptom score.

Key Takeaway Correlations don’t imply causation, but they help prioritize which student-level factors are most consistently linked to outcomes. Exercise and weeknight sleep align with lower symptom scores, while multiple substance-use measures align with higher scores. Routines are a realistic “first step” support lever when stigma delays care.

Exercise: a gradual improvement pattern

Median anxiety and depression scores decrease as weekly exercise increases.

Key Takeaway Median anxiety (GAD-7) and depression (PHQ-9) scores decrease as weekly exercise increases, showing a clear step down pattern from low activity to high activity groups. The difference is visible for both conditions, suggesting that higher activity levels are associated with lower symptom burden rather than affecting only one outcome. Exercise is a low-barrier, low-stigma intervention point small increases in activity may meaningfully align with better mental health outcomes at the population level.

Sleep and symptom severity

Symptoms are most reduced around a middle range of weeknight sleep, with both extremes being more associated with greater mental health concerns

Key Takeaway Anxiety (GAD-7) and depression (PHQ-9) scores are lowest around a mid-range of weeknight sleep (roughly ~8–9 hours), with higher average scores appearing at both shorter and longer sleep durations. This “Goldilocks” pattern suggests that sleep can be both a protective routine and a warning signal extreme sleep amounts may reflect stress/overload on one end and fatigue or depressive symptoms on the other. Sleep is a practical, low-stigma intervention point, and students at sleep extremes may benefit from early check-ins and support before needs escalate.

Adjust substance-use levels and condition selection to see how predicted help-seeking changes in an interpretable way.

Key Takeaway This “out of 100” view makes therapy seeking tangible: each highlighted figure represents a student who sought therapy, and the rest represent students who did not. 06 | Data & methods

Data source and key characteristics

The data for this project comes from Healthy Minds Study (HMS), a large scale, web-based survey assessing college student mental health and help-seeking behaviours administered annually across participating U.S. colleges and universities. This study was initially developed at the University of Michigan School of Public Health and is a widely used dataset for understanding mental health trends in higher education. The data we have leverages is from 2007 till 2013 due to consistency across variables.

Measures

  • GAD-7 anxiety score + severity categories
  • PHQ-9 depression score + severity categories
  • Perceived need for care
  • Past-year treatment utilization (therapy and medication)
  • Barriers to care (multi-select, both no/reduced service access)
  • Demographic information

Limitations

  • Cross-sectional and self-reported survey responses
  • Potential selection bias (who responds / which schools participate)
  • Potential response bias (reporting of symptoms and barriers)

One thing to note is that since participation in the survey is voluntary at both the institutional and student levels, the data are cross-sectional and self-reported, which may cause certain selection and response bias. That being said, the scale of this study and its use of validated instruments makes it well-suited for analyzing disparities in mental health access and unmet treatment needs among college populations.
