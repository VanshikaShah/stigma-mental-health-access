05 | What can we do about it?

Everyday student habits that move with mental health and help-seeking

Stigma can make formal care feel hard to access, so this section highlights low barrier places where support can start: daily routines and behaviors that track with symptoms and patterns in who actually seeks therapy.

What tends to rise with symptoms and what tends to protect?

A high-level view of how lifestyle and substance use relate to anxiety and depression outcomes in this survey.

What stands out Correlations don’t imply causation, but they help prioritize which student-level factors are most consistently linked to outcomes. Exercise and weeknight sleep align with lower symptom scores, while multiple substance-use measures align with higher scores. Key takeaway: routines are a realistic “first step” support lever when stigma delays care.

Exercise: a gradual improvement pattern

Median anxiety and depression scores decrease as weekly exercise increases.

Median anxiety (GAD-7) and depression (PHQ-9) scores decrease as weekly exercise increases, showing a clear step down pattern from low activity to high activity groups. The difference is visible for both conditions, suggesting that higher activity levels are associated with lower symptom burden rather than affecting only one outcome. Key takeaway: Exercise is a low-barrier, low-stigma intervention point small increases in activity may meaningfully align with better mental health outcomes at the population level.

Sleep and symptom severity

Outcomes are lowest around a middle range of weeknight sleep, with worse scores at both extremes.

Anxiety (GAD-7) and depression (PHQ-9) scores are lowest around a mid-range of weeknight sleep (roughly ~8–9 hours), with higher average scores appearing at both shorter and longer sleep durations. This “Goldilocks” pattern suggests that sleep can be both a protective routine and a warning signal extreme sleep amounts may reflect stress/overload on one end and fatigue or depressive symptoms on the other. Sleep is a practical, low-stigma intervention point, and students at sleep extremes may benefit from early check-ins and support before needs escalate.

Help-seeking differences by lifestyle factors

Therapy-seeking varies across lifestyle contexts and differs between anxiety and depression.

Help-seeking is not uniform across groups some contexts show larger gaps than others. Key takeaway: outreach should be tailored to where help-seeking is lowest, not only where symptoms are highest.

06 • Data & methods

Data source and key characteristics

Data comes from the Healthy Minds Study (HMS), a web-based survey of college student mental health and help-seeking.

Measures

  • GAD-7 anxiety score + severity categories
  • PHQ-9 depression score + severity categories
  • Perceived need for care
  • Past-year treatment utilization
  • Barriers to care (multi-select)

Limitations

  • Cross-sectional and self-reported survey responses
  • Potential selection bias (who responds / which schools participate)
  • Potential response bias (reporting of symptoms and barriers)
