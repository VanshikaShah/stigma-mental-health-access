Using Healthy Minds Study survey data, we explore how stigma keeps students from getting the support they need. We highlight symptom prevalence, the care gap, and actions that could improve access.

02 | Why Stigma Stands Out

Stigma-related barriers remain prominent

This section focuses on reported barriers to care and highlights the role stigma plays relative to other obstacles like time, cost, and access.

Barriers to care (No Services vs Reduced Services to care)

Start with Condition Type + Service Access + Severity. Use Race/Ethnicity and finances for drilldowns.

Key takeaway Across both groups of students who received no services and those who received reduced services, stigma-related barriers remain highly reported, suggesting that the treatment gap is not explained by logistics alone. Structural issues matter, but concerns like privacy, self-reliance, and perceived judgment continue to shape whether students seek and continue care. Access expands treatment only partially unless stigma-related barriers are addressed.

Key takeaway Across groups, perceived need is consistently higher than care received, creating a visible treatment gap that varies by sexuality and race/ethnicity. In several subgroups, the gap remains large even when need is high, suggesting that access barriers persist beyond symptoms alone. It is also seen that LGBTQ+ community consistently face higher percentage of reporting demographic barriers. Differences in the treatment gap across demographics point to the importance of culturally and identity responsive services, not just increasing overall capacity.
05 | What can we do about it?

Everyday student habits that move with mental health and help-seeking

Stigma can make formal care feel hard to access, so this section highlights low barrier places where support can start: daily routines and behaviors that track with symptoms—and patterns in who actually seeks therapy.

What tends to rise with symptoms—and what tends to protect?

A high-level view of how lifestyle and substance use relate to anxiety and depression outcomes in this survey.

What stands out Correlations don’t imply causation, but they help prioritize which student-level factors are most consistently linked to outcomes. Exercise and weeknight sleep align with lower symptom scores, while multiple substance-use measures align with higher scores. Key takeaway: routines are a realistic “first step” support lever when stigma delays care.

06 • Data & methods

Data source and key characteristics

Data comes from the Healthy Minds Study (HMS), a web-based survey of college student mental health and help-seeking.

Measures

  • GAD-7 anxiety score + severity categories
  • PHQ-9 depression score + severity categories
  • Perceived need for care
  • Past-year treatment utilization
  • Barriers to care (multi-select)

Limitations

  • Cross-sectional and self-reported survey responses
  • Potential selection bias (who responds / which schools participate)
  • Potential response bias (reporting of symptoms and barriers)
